Impacts of North Atlantic biases on the upper troposphere/lower stratosphere over the extratropical North Pacific

The North Atlantic Ocean plays an important role in regulating various regional climate patterns because of the presence of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) and its associated heat transport. On multidecadal timescales, particularly during boreal winter, changes in North Atlantic sea surface temperatures (SSTs) have been argued to lead to in-phase changes in North Pacific SSTs. Owing to the limited availability of long observational records, much of the evidence for this inter-basin linkage is derived from climate model simulations.

Read more

A persistent issue in many coupled climate models is the presence of cold SST biases in the North Atlantic, which are often associated with biases in the simulated strength and structure of the AMOC. The SST biases in the Atlantic can induce SSTs biases in the North Pacific. However, the extent to which such biases affect the upper atmosphere, particularly the upper troposphere‚Äìlower stratosphere (UTLS) over the North Pacific, has not been explored. In this work, we assess the influence of North Atlantic oceanic biases on the UTLS over the extratropical North Pacific.

Previous studies have commonly employed SST nudging to correct North Atlantic SST biases and study their impacts. However, SST nudging does not correct ocean circulation biases (e.g., unrealistic North Atlantic Current pathways and weaker return flow in the deep ocean), and residual SST biases often remain. This raises questions about the degree to which SST-nudged experiments capture the full impact of Atlantic biases. Here, we use robust diagnostic calculations in which both ocean temperature and salinity throughout the column are nudged toward observed climatology to produce a more realistic ocean circulation and effectively correct the cold biases in the North Atlantic. We compare these results with SST-nudging experiments to evaluate how differences in the treatment of ocean circulation affect the simulated impacts on the North Pacific.

Interhemispheric footprint on Indian monsoon floods

The Indian summer monsoon rainfall is often believed to be affected by the leading climate patterns such as the El Ni√±o Southern Oscillation (ENSO). Typically, large-scale droughts over India are associated with El Ni√±o (i.e., positive phase of ENSO), while floods with La Ni√±a (negative phase of ENSO). However, this connection does not always hold, some major floods over India have occured even without La Ni√±a conditions. Here, we try to comprehend- (i) the rainfall characteristics of such floods, and (ii) the dynamical conditions that contribute to these large-scale floods over India.

Read more

Using observations and reanalysis, we show that the floods in the absence of La Ni√±a follow a unique evolution pattern within the monsoon season itself. Instead of steady rainfall throughout the season, these floods are driven by an intense burst of rain late in the season (blue line in the Figure on left). This late-season surge results from a complex interhemispheric interaction of (i) a wave train from the northern midlatitudes that curves southward into the subtropics and (ii) an enhanced low-level jet which is linked to a stronger pressure difference between Madagascar and the Indian subcontinent. Together, these processes trigger heavy rainfall over India. Our findings reveal a new dynamical pathway that explains that the large-scale monsoon floods can result from interactions between hemispheres, offering fresh insight beyond the traditional tropical ENSO-based view.
